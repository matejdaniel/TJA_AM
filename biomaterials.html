What is biomaterial?#

A biomaterial is broadly defined as any material, surface, or construct that interacts with biological systems and is used or adapted for a medical application. These materials are specifically chosen to create artificial devices that can safely, reliably, and economically replace or support a part or function of the body while being accepted by the body.

Key aspects of biomaterials include:

  • Origin and Composition

    • They can be natural or man-made. Historically, early biomaterials were “off-the-shelf” commercial polymers and metals initially developed for other applications and then adapted for medical use. Today, they encompass a wide range of materials, including metals and alloys, ceramics, polymers, and composites. Increasingly, biological tissues and materials derived from biology are also included.

    • They can be reengineered into various forms such as coatings, fibers, films, foams, textiles, or molded or machined fragments.

  • Purpose and Function

    • Biomaterials are employed in medical practice to promote the healing of human tissues, regenerate tissues, or replace lost or damaged tissue due to injury or disease. This includes applications like joint replacements, bone plates, dental implants, heart valves, and skin repair devices.

    • Their function can be benign (e.g., a heart valve) or bioactive, involving more interactive purposes such as hydroxyapatite-coated hip implants.

    • They are designed to mimic the tissues they replace or interact with, combining physical and chemical properties with minimal foreign body host response.

  • Biocompatibility

    • Biocompatibility is a fundamental requirement for any material used as an implant.

    • It is defined as the ability of a material to perform with an appropriate host response in a specific application. This means the material should execute its anticipated activities without causing any unwanted local or systemic influences in the body, such as toxic, inflammatory, or allergic reactions.

    • The interactive physical attributes of a biomaterial with the adjacent host principally determine its biocompatibility. This interaction begins at the surface with water-surface interaction, followed by protein adsorption, and then cell adherence and spreading.

  • Evolution of the Concept

    • Initially, the field focused on developing bioinert materials that exhibited minimal interaction with surrounding tissues.

    • Over time, advancements led to second-generation biomaterials that were bioactive, bioresorbable, and biodegradable, designed to elicit a specific biological response and form stable chemical bonds with natural bone. These can be progressively removed from the body after serving their purpose, potentially avoiding secondary surgeries.

    • The third generation of biomaterials aims to stimulate the regeneration of functional or living tissues at a molecular level, rather than just repairing or replacing diseased parts. This involves designing materials with specific cellular responses and utilizing micro and nanofabrication techniques for advanced tissue engineering.

    • The latest definition (2018) states that “a biomaterial is designed to take a form that can direct, through interactions with living systems, the course of any therapeutic/diagnostic procedure”.

Metallic biomaterials#

Metallic materials are widely used in biomedical implants due to their superior mechanical properties, such as high strength-to-weight ratio, excellent corrosion and fatigue resistance, and longevity. They are indispensable for applications requiring load-bearing capabilities, including joint replacement surgeries, musculoskeletal repairs, and orthodontics. However, metallic implants are not perfectly biocompatible and often require modifications to enhance their interaction with biological tissues and minimize adverse effects.

The suitability of a biomaterial for an implant depends on its ability to perform its intended function without causing unwanted local or systemic effects in the body, which is broadly defined as biocompatibility. This includes mechanical biocompatibility (proper placement and stability), biological compatibility (tissue-material interaction), and histocompatibility (minimizing adverse inflammatory and immune responses, and blood compatibility). Many metals currently used in medical devices were initially developed for the aerospace industry, prioritizing mechanical properties over biological compatibility. Consequently, a critical focus in biomaterials research is to improve the biocompatibility of these metallic implants for the next generation of medical devices.

Here is a comprehensive overview of the types of metals used for implants, along with their advantages and disadvantages:

1. Stainless Steel (SS)#

Stainless steel (SS), particularly AISI 316L, has a long history of application in medical devices due to its favorable mechanical properties and cost-effectiveness.

  • Applications: SS is commonly used for short-term and temporary implants like bone curettes, hemostats, orthodontic pliers, scalpels, root elevators, dental burs, solid instrument handles, pins, and fasteners. It is also found in hypodermic needles and sterilizing units. High-nitrogen SS is favored in some regions for cemented femoral stems.

  • Advantages:

    • Low Cost: SS implants are among the cheapest and easiest to manufacture.

    • Good Machinability: They are easily machinable.

    • Mechanical Strength: SS exhibits superb mechanical properties, including high yield strength, ultimate tensile strength, high wear resistance, good fatigue resistance, and ductility.

    • Corrosion Resistance: It offers good short-term corrosion resistance, especially due to the formation of a chromium oxide layer.

  • Disadvantages:

    • Long-Term Corrosion: SS can corrode in long-term applications, leading to pitting and crevice corrosion, which can disrupt implant stability and cause deep pits on the metal surface.

    • Stress Shielding: Its high elastic modulus (~200 GPa) is significantly stiffer than human bone (10-30 GPa), which can induce stress shielding. This effect shifts stress to nearby bones, leading to bone resorption and potential implant loosening.

    • Toxicity and Allergic Reactions: SS contains Nickel (Ni) and Chromium (Cr), which can cause toxicity, allergic reactions, and inflammations in patients. The release of metal ions can also lead to metallosis, pseudotumors, and periprosthetic bone loss.

    • Infection Risk: The release of iron from stainless steel can act as an iron source for bacteria, potentially favoring bacterial infections.

    • Limited Durability: SS is generally only suitable for temporary implants.

2. Titanium (Ti) and Titanium Alloys#

Titanium and its alloys, such as commercially pure titanium (CpTi) and Ti-6Al-4V (Ti64), are among the most widely used metals for biomedical implants. Beta-Titanium (β-Ti) alloys are also gaining prominence.

  • Applications: Ti and its alloys are extensively used in load-bearing applications like total hip and knee arthroplasty (femoral stems), fracture fixation devices (plates and screws), dental implants, spinal implants, pacemakers, and surgical instruments.

  • Advantages:

    • Excellent Biocompatibility: Ti and its alloys are considered highly biocompatible, bioinert, and non-toxic, readily forming a stable, protective oxide layer (titanium dioxide) on their surface that enhances their interaction with biological tissues and provides remarkable corrosion resistance.

    • High Strength-to-Weight Ratio: They offer a superior strength-to-weight ratio, making them ideal for sustained load-bearing applications.

    • Good Corrosion and Fatigue Resistance: They exhibit excellent resistance to corrosion and fatigue, crucial for long-term implant success in the physiological environment.

    • Lower Elastic Modulus: Compared to SS and Co-Cr alloys, Ti alloys have a relatively lower Young’s modulus (e.g., 104-113 GPa for Ti6Al4V, 110 GPa for Cp-Ti). β-Ti alloys, specifically, can achieve an even lower modulus (e.g., Ti–24Nb–4Zr–8Sn with 46–55 GPa), which significantly improves mechanical biocompatibility by reducing the stress shielding effect.

    • Osseointegration: They facilitate good osseointegration, meaning the direct structural and functional connection between living bone and the surface of a load-bearing implant.

  • Disadvantages:

    • Cost: Ti and its alloys can be expensive.

    • Wear Resistance: Some Ti alloys, like Ti6Al4V, have lower wear resistance.

    • Toxicity Concerns (Ti6Al4V): The introduction of Aluminum (Al) and Vanadium (V) in Ti6Al4V has raised questions due to their inherent toxicity from free ion leaching, with excessive Al linked to Alzheimer’s disease and V to neurological conditions. Newer β-Ti alloys like Ti-29Nb-13Ta-4.6Zr (TNTZ) aim to eliminate these toxic elements.

    • Allergic Reactions: Despite general biocompatibility, Ti alloys can cause allergic tissue reactions, particularly due to fretting. Cases of titanium hypersensitivity have been reported, leading to persistent inflammation, osteolysis, and pain.

    • Diagnostic Interference: Over time, titanium implants can obstruct diagnostic imaging, making visualization of surrounding tissues difficult.

    • Fibrous Capsule Formation: The contrast between Ti alloy composition and human bone may lead to the formation of a fibrous capsule around the implant, hindering strong chemical bonding during osseointegration.

3. Cobalt-Chromium (Co-Cr) Alloys#

Co-Cr alloys, such as CoCrMo, have been used in orthopedic implants since the 1930s and are known for their high performance.

  • Applications: They are widely used for permanent implants, particularly femoral heads in hip replacements, and other articulating surfaces where wear resistance and low friction are critical. They are also applied in dentistry and cardiovascular stents.

  • Advantages:

    • High Wear Resistance: Co-Cr alloys exhibit the highest wear resistance among metals used for biomedical implants, making them suitable for applications undergoing friction and movement.

    • Excellent Corrosion and Fatigue Resistance: They offer high long-term corrosion and fatigue resistance.

    • Mechanical Properties: Possess high strength, hardness, and creep resistance, making them durable for load-bearing applications.

    • Better Castability: Exhibit better castability compared to some other metallic biomaterials.

  • Disadvantages:

    • Processing Difficulty and Cost: They are difficult to machine, leading to expensive processing.

    • High Elastic Modulus: Similar to SS, Co-Cr alloys have a high elastic modulus (200-280 GPa), which can lead to stress shielding and bone overload.

    • Toxicity and Allergic Reactions: The presence of Ni and Cr can cause toxicity, allergic reactions, and inflammations. The release of metal ions and wear debris can lead to adverse local tissue reactions, pseudotumors, necrosis of soft tissues, and systemic toxicity (e.g., cobalt poisoning), and osteolysis. Cobalt and chromium ions exceeding certain blood thresholds are linked to serious health issues.

    • Implant Loosening: High implant loosening rates have been reported.

    • Osteoclast Stimulation: Metals like cobalt and chromium can stimulate osteoclast activity, leading to increased bone resorption.

4. Magnesium (Mg) Alloys#

Magnesium alloys are considered a promising class of biodegradable or resorbable metals, designed to degrade over time within the body, eliminating the need for secondary removal surgeries.

  • Applications: Primarily explored for cardiovascular stents and temporary orthopedic fixation devices like screws, pins, and plates. Also applicable for replacement joints and spinal discs.

  • Advantages:

    • Biodegradability: They offer biodegradable properties, allowing them to resorb into the body after the healing period, thereby avoiding follow-up surgeries for implant removal.

    • Mechanical Matching: Their elastic modulus (~40 GPa) is closer to that of human bone (10-30 GPa) compared to other metallic implants, which helps in reducing the stress shielding effect.

    • Natural Occurrence: Magnesium is a naturally occurring element in the human body (50-60% resides in bone), and its degradation byproducts (Mg ions) are non-toxic, minimizing rejection risk.

    • Osteopromotive Properties: They show promise in promoting osteoblast activity and bone healing.

    • Reduced Hydrogen Evolution (with Zn): Alloying with Zinc can reduce hydrogen gas formation during degradation, a common issue with pure Mg.

    • Antibacterial Potential: Zinc alloyed Mg stents can provide inherent antibacterial characteristics.

  • Disadvantages:

    • High Degradation Rate: Pure Mg and its alloys can degrade rapidly in vivo, making them unfit for long-term load-bearing applications.

    • Hydrogen Evolution: The primary cathodic reaction during Mg alloy corrosion is hydrogen evolution, which can create subcutaneous gas bubbles, interfering with the healing process and sometimes requiring puncture procedures.

    • Soft and Malleable: Mg is naturally soft and malleable.

    • Corrosion Challenges: Accelerated degradation due to corrosion can lead to premature implant weakening. Higher calcium content can lead to irregular corrosion. Special surface treatments are often required to control degradation.

5. Tantalum (Ta) and Tantalum Alloys#

Tantalum is a transition metal known for its chemical stability and biocompatibility, increasingly explored for standalone orthopedic implants.

  • Applications: Historically used in secondary medical applications like suturing wires, ligation clips, and staples. More recently, it’s used as a coating on other metals (e.g., Ti implants) and carbon composites, and in porous forms (e.g., “Trabecular Metal”) for orthopedic implants like femoral stems and acetabular cups.

  • Advantages:

    • Exceptional Biocompatibility: Highly biocompatible and non-cytotoxic, forming a protective oxide layer on its surface that prevents corrosive effects from physiological fluids. It’s believed to mitigate inflammatory processes and immune reactions.

    • Excellent Corrosion Resistance: Offers superior corrosion resistance.

    • Enhanced Cell Response: When coated on Ti, Ta coatings show a higher osteoblast response and increased surface energy, promoting cellular interactions and making surfaces more hydrophilic.

    • Osseointegration: Porous tantalum structures promote superior osseointegration and bone ingrowth, due to their three-dimensional, interconnected pore structures. Early-stage osseointegration and new bone formation have been observed in vivo.

    • Alloying Benefits: Alloying Ta with Ti can enhance strength and reduce the elastic modulus of Ti alloys while providing better corrosion resistance.

  • Disadvantages:

    • High Density: It is a comparatively heavy metal (~16 g/cc), which can be a drawback for bulk implants.

    • Soft and Ductile: Bulk implants made solely of Ta may not withstand the patient’s regular activities due to its softness and ductility.

    • Processing Challenges: Has a very high melting temperature (3200 K), making conventional furnace processing difficult and expensive.

    • Cost: Significantly more expensive than titanium (up to 10 times).

    • Limited Biological Response: While non-cytotoxic, Ta alone doesn’t actively contribute to biological responses; its biocompatibility is primarily attributed to its inertness.

    • Potential Toxicity: Some evidence suggests a risk of local sarcomas and toxicity to alveolar cells.

    • Bio-Tolerant Classification: Categorized as bio-tolerant, meaning it can lead to fibrous tissue encapsulation at the implant interface.

6. Niobium (Nb) Alloys#

Niobium alloys, particularly when combined with Titanium and Zirconium (e.g., Ti-13Nb-13Zr), have gained prominence as alternatives to conventional Co-Cr alloys.

  • Applications: Used in orthopedic implants, especially for total hip arthroplasty (THA).

  • Advantages:

    • High Biocompatibility and Hypoallergenic: Nb alloys exhibit excellent biocompatibility and hypoallergenic qualities, meaning they do not cause negative bodily reactions or allergic responses, unlike some other metals. Niobium is believed to reduce allergy to metallic implants.

    • Enhanced Mechanical Properties: They offer increased fatigue strength and corrosion resistance, crucial for the longevity of hip replacements.

    • Reduced Ion Release: Designed to minimize the discharge of hazardous ions, addressing a common problem with other metallic materials.

  • Disadvantages:

    • Higher Production Cost: The production of these advanced alloys is costlier than traditional materials.

    • Limited Long-Term Data: Requires further long-term clinical data to fully validate their benefits across various patient populations.

7. Nickel-Titanium (NiTi) / Nitinol#

Nitinol, a nickel-titanium alloy, is a prominent shape memory alloy known for its unique thermomechanical properties.

  • Applications: Has the most prolonged and extensive history of application in dentistry. Also used in orthopedics, maxillofacial, and cardiovascular applications. Specific uses include enhancing the initial stability of uncemented femoral stems and in modular acetabular insert revision systems due to its ability to expand and conform.

  • Advantages:

    • Shape Memory and Pseudoelasticity: Its unique properties, including the shape memory effect and pseudoelasticity, allow it to simulate the biomechanical properties of natural tissues, making it ideal for certain applications.

    • Improved Load-Bearing: Can reduce stiffness in proportion to bone, leading to superior load-bearing in the healing mechanism and faster bone recovery compared to SS, Ti, and Co-Cr.

    • Cellular Tolerance: Well tolerated by osteosarcoma, osteoblasts, osteoblast-like MG63 cells, and human peripheral blood lymphocytes, showing no cytotoxicity or genotoxic activity for human peripheral blood lymphocytes.

    • Flexibility: Beta titanium wire (a type of NiTi) offers twice the flexibility and half the force of stainless steel.

  • Disadvantages:

    • Nickel Toxicity and Allergic Reactions: The high amount of nickel can cause carcinogenic and allergic reactions in patients. Nickel is a common allergen, and its release from wear debris can lead to hypersensitivity reactions and other biocompatibility issues.

    • Permanent Distortion: Overstressing NiTi wire can cause permanent distortion.

    • Micromotion: Challenges related to micromotion at the bone–implant interface can still persist.

    • Research Gap: There is limited literature specifically on NiTi alloys’ cell-material interaction.

8. Other Metals and Alloys#

  • Noble Metals (Gold, Silver, Platinum, Iridium, Palladium):

    • Applications: Gold has been used in ancient dental work and modern dental implants, prosthetic devices, and drug delivery systems. Platinum is found in medical implants, electrodes, and catheters. Silver is used for its antimicrobial properties in wound dressings and catheters.

    • Advantages: Highly corrosion-resistant and generally considered bioinert (Gold, Platinum). Silver exhibits antimicrobial properties.

    • Disadvantages: Can be susceptible to corrosion in vivo/in vitro environments. Even precious metals can become immunogenic or cause sensitization. Silver can react with some biological substances.

  • Bulk Metallic Glass (BMG): These amorphous alloys are being considered for biomedical implants.

    • Advantages: Offer favorable toughness, high tensile strength, elasticity, low internal friction, and sufficient resistance to corrosion and wear.

    • Disadvantages: The absence of crystallinity poses production challenges.

  • Oxidized Zirconium (OxZr): A zirconium-based alloy that is heat-treated to form a strong, wear-resistant ceramic surface while retaining a metallic core.

    • Applications: Increasingly popular in THA, especially for femoral heads, particularly for younger patients or those with metal allergies due to its low ion release.

    • Advantages: Combines the toughness and fracture resistance of metals with the wear resistance and biocompatibility of ceramics. Drastically reduces wear rates of polyethylene liners and offers improved biocompatibility.

    • Disadvantages: Very high cost.

  • Zinc (Zn) Alloys: Emerging as a bioresorbable alternative to Mg-based alloys.

    • Applications: Explored for cardiovascular stents and load-bearing bone implants.

    • Advantages: Bioresorbable, eliminating the long-term presence of foreign material. Can exhibit mechanical properties comparable to medical-grade Ti. Zinc addition can reduce hydrogen gas formation during degradation compared to pure Mg. Possesses inherent antibacterial characteristics.

    • Disadvantages: Still in nascent research and evaluation stages. May require specific alloying strategies or post-fabrication thermal treatments for mechanical biocompatibility. Concerns exist regarding late stenosis and/or thrombosis in stent applications, as well as potential for moderate inflammation and neointimal hyperplasia.

Polymeric materials#

Polymeric materials play a significant role in the field of joint implants, particularly in orthopaedic applications such as total hip arthroplasty (THA) and knee replacements. They are widely recognized for their distinct properties that allow for seamless integration with the body and reduction of adverse reactions like inflammation or toxicity.

Evolution and General Characteristics of Polymeric Biomaterials#

Historically, polymers began to be introduced as acceptable biomaterials in the 1940s, alongside stainless steel and cobalt-chromium alloys. Early synthetic materials like rubber and Pyrex glass were experimented with for hip prostheses, though they proved insufficient for the mechanical demands of hip joint replacement. The development of polymeric biomaterials for implants has continuously aimed to improve patient quality of life by providing strong, inert, and biocompatible solutions for injured or infected bones.

Polymers offer tailorable characteristics with exceptional flexibility in their chemical and physical surface behavior, allowing precise regulation of bulk properties such as porosity, mechanical properties, biocompatibility, and biodegradation. While they are broadly used as implants in orthopaedic and tissue engineering applications, their main drawbacks historically included higher degradation rates and low mechanical strength when used alone. These limitations have driven the development of composites with metals or ceramic materials to overcome these challenges for advanced biomedical applications.

Types of Polymeric Materials in Joint Implants#

Several polymeric materials have been extensively studied and applied in joint implants:

  1. Ultra-High Molecular Weight Polyethylene (UHMWPE):

    • Description and History: UHMWPE is a high-density, semi-crystalline polymer introduced by Sir John Charnley in the 1960s to replace Polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE), which exhibited significant wear issues. Its structure consists of long chains of ethylene with a crystallinity of 50–55%, contributing to its exceptional wear resistance and durability.

    • Application: UHMWPE is a widely consumed polymer in bioimplants due to its tribological properties, primarily used as acetabular liners in hip replacements. It has also been applied in knee replacements as a tibial cushion and patella.

    • Properties: It offers superior mechanical properties, enhanced modulus, and creep resistance compared to other polymeric biomaterials. It provides good impact absorption and has a low friction coefficient.

    • Challenges: Despite its advantages, UHMWPE releases wear debris over prolonged periods, leading to adverse tissue reactions and osteolysis (bone loss), which can cause implant loosening and early failure. This issue is a primary concern in implant longevity.

  2. Highly Cross-Linked Polyethylene (HXLPE):

    • Development: HXLPE was developed to address the wear issues of conventional UHMWPE. It is primarily produced by subjecting the material to ionizing radiation (e.g., gamma rays), which greatly improves its resistance to wear and lowers the generation of wear particles. Subsequent thermal treatments like annealing and remelting further enhance wear resistance and oxidation stability by reducing free radicals.

    • Application: HXLPE is frequently used in orthopaedic surgery, especially for joint replacement components like acetabular cups. It is often combined with larger diameter femoral heads to improve implant stability and reduce the risk of dislocation.

    • Properties: It offers significantly higher wear resistance than uncrosslinked polyethylene, improved oxidative stability, and a substantial reduction in wear particle production.

    • Challenges: While superior to conventional PE, high cross-linking density may compromise mechanical properties such as hardness and rigidity. Oxidation during storage can degrade the material, and there are concerns about fatigue fractures and oxidation in certain HXLPE designs, particularly with thin liners. Incorporating antioxidants like vitamin E is a strategy to enhance its oxidative stability and durability.

  3. Polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA):

    • Application: PMMA is widely known as bone cement in THA and other orthopaedic applications. It acts as a grout rather than an adhesive, providing immediate stability to cemented implants.

    • Properties: It demonstrates superior osseointegration and lacks interim fibrous tissue around cemented elements.

    • Challenges: PMMA can face microfractures and release cement particles, causing a localized foreign-body response and limited biological response. Its polymerization is exothermic, releasing heat that can cause thermal necrosis if not managed carefully. Poor biodegradability can also lead to complications like intramedullary hypertension, highlighting the need for improved biodegradable cements.

  4. Polyetheretherketone (PEEK):

    • Description: PEEK is a high-performance polymer increasingly popular as a substitute for metals and ceramics in joint replacements.

    • Application: It is primarily investigated for acetabular liners and femoral stems in THA. It is also used in spinal and orthopaedic implants due to its radiolucency.

    • Properties: PEEK offers exceptional mechanical qualities, including high tensile strength, biocompatibility, and chemical resistance. Its modulus of elasticity is closer to human bone compared to metals, which helps mitigate stress shielding. Its radiolucency aids post-operative imaging.

    • Challenges: Research is ongoing to increase its resistance to wear and long-term performance under cyclic loading conditions. Carbon fibre-reinforced PEEK (CFR-PEEK) is being explored to enhance mechanical properties, offering a robust and lightweight option that better mimics natural bone mechanics.

  5. Polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE):

    • History and Challenges: Also known as Teflon, PTFE was one of the earliest polymers used in orthopaedic implants due to its low friction coefficient and chemical resistance. However, it showed poor wear resistance under the high-stress, repeated conditions of the hip joint, leading to rapid deterioration and wear particle formation. These particles often caused inflammatory responses and osteolysis, frequently necessitating revision operations. Due to these limitations, UHMWPE replaced PTFE in many applications.

  6. Polyamide (Nylon):

    • History and Challenges: Nylon was investigated for its strong mechanical qualities, including high tensile strength, elasticity, and wear resistance, making it a desirable option for articulating surfaces of hip implants. Early clinical outcomes were promising in the short term, but its long-term performance and potential for wear debris generation remain considerations.

  7. Polyurethane (PU):

    • Application and Properties: PU is a versatile biomaterial in THA due to its elasticity, flexibility, and biocompatibility. Its elastic nature helps absorb and distribute stresses more uniformly, reducing stress concentration on the implant and surrounding bone tissue, which is beneficial for the hip joint’s dynamic loading. It can also replicate the mechanical characteristics of natural cartilage. It can be molded into various shapes, including solid articulating surfaces or porous scaffolds.

    • Challenges: PU may deteriorate over time, leading to a decrease in mechanical strength and potential implant failure. Long-term interaction with biological tissues is still under investigation for potential inflammation or negative reactions.

Biodegradable Polymers#

While many polymers are non-biodegradable for permanent applications, a distinct class of biodegradable polymers exists for temporary implants, avoiding secondary surgeries for removal after healing. Examples include:

  • Polyglycolic Acid (PGA): A synthetic polymer with high crystallinity and degradation rate due to its hydrophilism, though this high degradation can lead to a decrease in mechanical strength after implantation.

  • Polylactic Acid (PLA): Can be synthesized from natural sources and, along with PGA, is used for degradable sutures and orthopaedic applications. It can be 3D printed into scaffolds with customized mechanical properties and biological functions for bone tissue engineering.

  • Poly-ε-caprolactone (PCL): An aliphatic polyester used in long-term implants and drug delivery systems due to its crystallinity and permeability. It offers high flexibility and shaping options.

  • Polyhydroxybutyrate (PHB): Another biodegradable polymer, although its brittleness limits practical applications alone; it’s often blended with other polymers.

  • Polydioxanone (PPD): Used for fracture internal fixation and in various forms like films and coatings due to its biodegradability and flexibility.

These biodegradable polymers are essential for applications like bone screws, bone fixation objects, sutures, and scaffolds. However, challenges remain in precisely controlling their degradation rates and addressing potential inflammation in acidic mediums.

Challenges and Future Directions#

The main challenges associated with polymeric implant materials include:

  • Mechanical Properties: Their strength and stiffness may not always meet the demands for load-bearing applications, leading to potential failure or reduced longevity.

  • Wear Debris and Osteolysis: Many polymers, especially earlier generations, release wear particles that trigger inflammatory responses and bone resorption (osteolysis), leading to implant loosening.

  • Long-term Stability and Degradation: Ensuring long-term stability and preventing degradation or wear over time remains a significant concern for both non-biodegradable and biodegradable polymers.

  • Immune Responses: Polymers can generate immunogenic reactions, although modern research aims to minimize these.

Future directions in polymeric biomaterials for joint implants involve the development of advanced composites, such as fiber-reinforced PEEK, which aim for enhanced wear resistance and outstanding biocompatibility. Research also focuses on advanced and innovative polymers that can overcome the drawbacks of conventional materials by decreasing oxidative deterioration and improving wear resistance. The goal is to create more reliable and longer-lasting prostheses with optimized stress distribution and fiber-matrix bonding. Furthermore, the integration of additive manufacturing (3D printing) with smart polymers offers new possibilities for developing responsive and patient-specific therapeutic platforms.

Ceramic materials#

Ceramic materials, often referred to as bioceramics when used in biomedical applications, are a crucial class of biomaterials extensively utilized in joint implants and other orthopaedic applications. Their distinct properties allow for seamless integration with the body and are designed to reduce adverse reactions like inflammation or toxicity.

Evolution and General Characteristics of Ceramic Biomaterials#

The use of ceramics in medicine has a long history, dating back to the 10th century AD with calcium sulfate for healing broken bones. Modern bioceramics for implants emerged in the 1970s. Biomaterials have evolved through three generations, with bioceramics playing a significant role from the first generation (bioinert) to the second (bioactive and biodegradable) and third (stimulating specific cellular responses at the molecular level).

Bioceramics are typically crystalline, semi-crystalline, or amorphous materials specifically engineered for the restoration and replacement of diseased or damaged body components. They are known for their high hardness, wear resistance, and superb biocompatibility. Key properties include chemical stability, high elastic modulus, high strength, hardness, and low density. A significant advantage of ceramic materials is that they generally produce a negligible amount of wear debris compared to metal and polymer-based implants, which is crucial for long-term implant success.

Bone is the tissue that most frequently requires repair after blood. Bioceramics are widely used in bone reconstruction, serving as mouldable pastes (e.g., injectable cements for spine surgery), porous granules, stiff scaffolds for filling bone gaps, and monolithic devices. Their capacity to establish a stable connection with host tissue is crucial, with bioactive and bioresorbable ceramics offering a significant solution.

Classification of Bioceramics in Joint Implants#

Bioceramics are generally classified into three main types based on their interaction with the biological system:

  1. Bioinert Ceramics: These materials exhibit minimal or little interaction with their neighboring tissues, meaning they are chemically inactive in the human environment. Despite their inertness, they are widely used as fixation screws and wires in orthopaedic applications due to their physical properties. Examples include alumina (Al2O3), zirconia (ZrO2), and titania (TiO2).

    • Alumina (Al2O3):

      • Properties and Applications: Alumina ceramics are bioinert materials known for their high hardness, increased abrasion resistance, and chemical inertness. They possess excellent wear and corrosion resistance. Al2O3 is frequently used in total hip arthroplasty (THA) because of its low chemical reactivity, high hardness, wear resistance, and biocompatibility, making it well-suited for prosthetic articular surfaces. This helps lower the risk of osteolysis and wear caused by debris from prosthetic movement, reducing inflammation and debris formation. Alumina has applications in load-bearing contexts, hip prostheses, and the dental field. It is primarily utilized in the production of acetabular inserts and femoral heads, often in ceramic-on-ceramic (CoC) couplings or combined with highly cross-linked polyethylene (HXLPE). Its elastic modulus ranges from 300–370 GPa, and mechanical strength from 210–220 MPa.

      • History and Challenges: Alumina was first used in orthopaedics in the 1970s as a superior alternative to metallic and polymeric materials, which were prone to severe wear and debris production. While it significantly improved the durability and lifetime of hip implants, its limitations include low fracture toughness and inability to deform under stress. Noiri et al. observed no damage or rejection of alumina ceramics after four weeks of socket implantation, with enhanced tissue growth.

    • Zirconia (ZrO2):

      • Properties and Applications: Zirconia is widely used in prosthetic devices due to its excellent chemical properties, dimensional stability, high mechanical strength (900–1250 MPa), toughness, and high Young’s modulus (200–220 GPa). It is particularly advantageous over other ceramics due to its ability to undergo transformation toughening, where it changes phases under stress, effectively halting crack propagation and increasing longevity in load-bearing applications. Zirconia ceramics are primarily used in total hip replacements, especially for femoral heads. Small additions of yttria (Y2O3) are commonly used to stabilize ZrO2 in its tetragonal phase at room temperature, producing Yttria-Stabilized Tetragonal Zirconia Polycrystals (Y-TZPs), which are well-suited for acetabular and femoral heads due to their exceptional strength and toughness.

      • History and Challenges: ZrO2 was first used in orthopaedic implants in the 1980s. While generally considered biologically safe with high biocompatibility for various cell types, studies by Lilley have shown that zirconia can exhibit degradation strength in wet environments. Another concern with Y-TZP ceramics is the possibility of “Low-Temperature Deterioration” (LTD) or aging, where the material changes phase over time when exposed to bodily fluids, potentially leading to reduced mechanical properties and increased risk of implant failure. It can also be expensive.

    • Titania (TiO2):

      • Properties and Applications: Titanium oxide and its alloys are common biomaterials known for excellent mechanical properties, biocompatibility, and non-toxicity. Titania is used to help uniform stress distribution and is suitable for load-bearing applications with an elastic modulus of 110 GPa. TiO2 coatings support osteoblast growth and bone remodeling.

  2. Bioresorbable Ceramics: These materials are designed to slowly dissolve in the human system without causing toxic effects, with natural bone replacing them over time. This eliminates the need for secondary surgeries for implant removal.

    • Examples: Low-temperature heated hydroxyapatite (HAp) and tricalcium phosphate (TCP) are examples of bioresorbable ceramics. Other bioresorbable materials include calcium phosphate, calcium sulfate, and calcium carbonate.

    • Applications: Bioresorbable ceramics are recommended for use in drug delivery systems and as temporary bone space fillers. They are crucial for applications like bone screws, bone fixation objects, and scaffolds.

    • Challenges: The degradation or dissolution rate of bioresorbable materials must align with the formation or regeneration rate of the surrounding tissues. Challenges include maintaining strength and stability until complete degradation and managing compositional changes in the body.

  3. Bioactive Ceramics: These bioceramics initiate proper interaction with the biological system by forming specific chemical bonds between the host tissues and the material’s active interface. They promote bone ingrowth with excellent biocompatibility and aid in avoiding fibrous layer formation and prosthesis failure.

    • Hydroxyapatite (HAp):

      • Properties and Applications: HAp is a widely consumed polymer in bioimplants due to its tribological properties, primarily used as acetabular liners in hip replacements. It is the mineral form of calcium apatites found in nature, similar in crystallographic structure and chemical composition to bone tissue. HAp has a slow resorption rate and is osteoconductive, promoting osteoblast proliferation, adhesion, migration, and differentiation, making it a prime coating material for dental and orthopaedic implants. It can form direct bonds to bone.

      • Limitations: HAp is not employed for load-bearing bone applications because of its poor mechanical properties, including low tensile and compressive strengths and intrinsic brittleness. To overcome this, HAp is often applied as a coating or mixed with other materials to improve its mechanical qualities. HAp nanoparticles have been found to induce cell apoptosis.

    • Tricalcium Phosphate (TCP):

      • Properties and Applications: TCP exhibits different polymorphic forms (α, β, γ, and super-α), with α and β TCP being preferred for biomedical devices. Both α-TCP and β-TCP have a Ca/P ratio of 1.5 and higher resorption rates than HAp. TCP is an osteoconductive material that promotes bone growth on the implant surface. It is used for bone graft remodelling and applications in orthopaedics, dentistry (capping agents, cleft palate), and vertical bone defects, often in conjunction with HAp.

      • Limitations: TCP has poor mechanical strength and low crack resistance.

    • Octacalcium Phosphates (OCP) and Dicalcium Phosphates (DCP): OCPs are stable at physiological pH and temperature and act as hydroxyapatite precursors. DCPs (like brushite) are biodegradable and convert into HAp in vivo, which limits their resorbability and degradation rate.

    • Bioactive Glass:

      • Properties and Applications: Bioactive glass, such as the original 45S5 Bioglass®, forms a direct bond with bone to encourage osteointegration and bone growth. Its composition (e.g., silica, sodium oxide, calcium oxide, phosphorus pentoxide) allows for the formation of a hydroxycarbonate layer resembling bone mineral, facilitating osteoblast attachment and new bone deposition. Bioactive glasses are used as bulk implants, scaffolds, and coatings on metallic implants. They show a high rate of bone formation and enhanced fixation.

      • Challenges: The major disadvantages of ceramics, including bioactive glass, are their brittleness and poor tensile properties, making them unsuitable for high-stress and load-bearing applications when used alone.

Ceramic Composites and Advanced Ceramics#

To overcome the inherent brittleness and low mechanical strength of standalone ceramics, composite materials have been developed. These composites combine ceramics with other materials like metals or polymers to enhance mechanical properties, fracture toughness, and overall performance in load-bearing applications.

  • Zirconia-Toughened Alumina (ZTA): ZTA combines the superior wear resistance of Al2O3 with the high toughness of ZrO2, typically incorporating 15–25% ZrO2 particles within an Al2O3 matrix. This composition significantly improves fracture toughness, making it ideal for high-stress applications like femoral heads in hip prostheses. Researchers developed ZTA in the late 1980s and early 1990s to address the brittleness of conventional ceramics.

  • Biolox Delta: This is a cutting-edge ceramic material designed to increase hardness and fracture resistance, especially beneficial for young, energetic patients. It is an advanced Al2O3-Y-TZP composite, offering a notable improvement in implant endurance and safety over conventional ceramics.

  • Ceramic-Metal Composites (Cermets): These composites, such as Ti-13Nb-13Zr-HA, are being explored to improve cell attachment and antimicrobial activity.

  • Gene-Activating Glasses and Nanoceramics: These are emerging areas of research. Gene-activating glasses aim to stimulate specific cellular responses for improved tissue regeneration. Nanoceramics, like nano-alumina formulations, focus on enhancing cell adhesion and proliferation while promoting bone formation at the nanometric level, potentially improving stress distribution and integration with bone.

Challenges and Future Directions#

Despite significant advancements, challenges remain for ceramic implant materials. Their brittleness and susceptibility to fracture, particularly in high-stress environments, limit their full utilization in load-bearing bones. Ensuring long-term stability and preventing degradation or wear over time are significant concerns for both non-biodegradable and biodegradable ceramics. Precise control over degradation rates for bioresorbable materials is also critical.

Future research in ceramic biomaterials for joint implants is focused on developing novel and improved materials with enhanced biological, tribological, chemical, and mechanical properties that mimic natural bones and tissues. This includes:

  • Developing new ceramic-based composite materials with negligible wear.

  • Further investigations into the structure-property-mechanism relationship of implant systems and precise regulation of nanoparticles in ceramic matrices.

  • Leveraging advanced manufacturing methods like 3D printing to create intricate, complex, and customized ceramic surfaces with optimized microstructures.

  • Addressing potential immune responses and minimizing wear debris-induced osteolysis.

  • The continued development of advanced and innovative ceramics that can overcome the drawbacks of conventional materials by decreasing oxidative deterioration and improving wear resistance is a key goal for creating more reliable and longer-lasting prostheses.

Composite materials#

Composite materials are a type of biomaterial consisting of two or more fundamentally different compounds, often combining the capabilities of diverse materials to create implants that are both strong and lightweight. They are designed to achieve a blend of high mechanical strength, flexibility, and biocompatibility, making them ideal for the demands of the human body.

General Properties and Advantages: The ability of composites to be customized to meet specific mechanical and biological requirements is a significant benefit. They can enhance stress distribution and reduce stress shielding by mimicking the mechanical behavior of natural bone, which helps preserve bone health around the implant. Composites offer an opportunity for a multi-design/multi-stage approach, allowing for better control of material and biological properties compared to using polymers, metals, or ceramics alone. The integration of advanced composites, such as metal-ceramic hybrids and antioxidant-infused polymers, holds significant potential for addressing challenges related to wear and long-term stability in implants.

Classification and Components: Synthetic biomaterials, including composites, are widely commercialized and applied in clinical settings. Composites can be broadly categorized based on their constituents:

  • Ceramic-based Composites: These are often prepared using bioinert and bioactive ceramics, offering excellent biocompatibility and mechanical strength. Examples include alumina/zirconia composites, which have shown good osteointegration. Hydroxyapatite (HAP) ceramics, which are similar to the inorganic phase of bone, are frequently used in composites to enhance osteoconductivity and bone bonding.

  • Metal Matrix Composites (MMCs): These combine metal matrices with reinforcing components to improve mechanical strength and wear resistance. MMCs are being explored to address key limitations of conventional metallic biomaterials, leveraging metals’ lightweight, high temperature, and corrosion resistance properties. Common types of MMCs for biomedical devices are reinforced with ceramics or other metals.

  • Polymer-based Composites: Polymers can be combined with metals or ceramic materials to overcome drawbacks like high degradation rates and low mechanical strength. This includes fiber-reinforced polymers.

Specific Composite Materials and Their Applications:

  1. Carbon Fiber-Reinforced Polyetheretherketone (CFR-PEEK):

    • Composition & Properties: CFR-PEEK consists of a PEEK polymer matrix reinforced with carbon fibers. This combination results in a robust and lightweight material that balances strength and flexibility. It is highly biocompatible and possesses high mechanical strength.

    • Advantages: CFR-PEEK is particularly effective in minimizing “stress shielding,” a common issue with traditional implants where stiffness differences between the prosthesis and bone tissue can lead to bone resorption. Its mechanical characteristics can more closely resemble those of natural bone, mitigating problems caused by these stiffness differences.

    • Applications: CFR-PEEK prostheses were first developed for Total Hip Arthroplasty (THA) in the 1980s and 1990s. They are primarily utilized in the production of THA femoral stems due to the benefits of decreased stiffness and enhanced mechanical compatibility.

    • Mechanical Properties (in THA): CFR-PEEK composites have an elastic modulus ranging from 70–150 GPa and mechanical strength from 600–1500 MPa. They exhibit low chemical reactivity and high wear resistance, making them a high-cost, high-performance option.

  2. Ultra-High-Molecular-Weight Polyethylene (UHMWPE) Composites:

    • Composition & Properties: UHMWPE is a high-density, semi-crystalline polymer with exceptional wear resistance and durability, making it suitable for THA. Recent advancements include reinforcing UHMWPE with hydroxyapatite (HAP) and carbon nanotubes (CNTs).

    • Advantages: These composites aim to provide improved mechanical qualities, such as increased tensile strength and wear resistance, while maintaining outstanding biocompatibility. CNTs, for example, are added to strengthen the structural integrity and resistance to mechanical stresses over time.

    • Applications: UHMWPE and its enhanced forms are crucial for bearing surfaces in THA, significantly extending implant longevity by reducing friction and the need for revision surgeries.

    • Mechanical Properties (in THA): UHMWPE + CNTs composites have an elastic modulus of 1–5 GPa and mechanical strength of 25–50 MPa. They show low chemical reactivity, high wear resistance, and high biocompatibility.

  3. Ceramic-Polymer Composites:

    • Composition & Properties: These composites use fiber reinforcements like HAP to improve the mechanical properties of ceramic-polymer combinations, resulting in robust, wear-resistant materials.

    • Applications: They can be used in prosthetic applications. Examples include calcium phosphate-coated magnesium alloys which showed enhanced bioactivity.

    • Performance: Alumina ceramics composites prepared with hydroxyapatite have been found to be highly bioactive with high adhesion strength. Animal experiments revealed excellent osseointegration with bone for HAP/Alumina composites. Zirconia (ZrO2) coatings on Ti–6Al–4V have demonstrated good corrosion resistance in simulated body fluid environments.

  4. Hydroxyapatite (HAP)-Silica Glass Composites:

    • Composition & Properties: HAP-Silica glass composites have been developed for bone tissue engineering, exhibiting excellent bioactive and biocompatible properties.

    • Performance: In vitro studies in SBF solutions and in vivo tests using pre-osteoblastic cell lines showed that HAP-Silica enhances cell adhesion, proliferation, and differentiation towards osteoblast phenotypes.

Role in Overcoming Limitations of Other Biomaterials: Composites address key challenges posed by standalone materials:

  • Ceramics: While ceramics offer high hardness, wear resistance, and superb biocompatibility with negligible wear debris, their major disadvantage is brittleness and poor tensile properties, limiting their use in high-stress, load-bearing applications. Composites enhance strength and fracture toughness, overcoming these limitations.

  • Polymers: Polymers face drawbacks such as higher degradation rates and low mechanical strength. Composites with metals or ceramics mitigate these issues, enabling advanced biomedical applications.

  • Metals: Metallic implants can suffer from issues like stress shielding (due to a mismatch between the implant’s stiffness and natural bone), corrosion, ion release, and potential allergic reactions. Composites can be designed to reduce stress shielding and enhance mechanical compatibility.

Fabrication Methods: Composites are manufactured using various techniques, including direct energy deposition (DED) and selective laser melting (SLM) for porous structures, and sol-gel methods for coatings. Research into powder bed fusion (PBF) for Mg-based and Ti-based MMCs is ongoing to optimize manufacturing processes.

Future Directions and Challenges: The current generation of biomaterials is considered “fourth generation,” combining more than two different phases to achieve better control over properties like mechanical strength, antibacterial activity, osteoconductivity, degradation rate, physicochemical characteristics, cell interaction, surface topography, and wettability.

Despite their advantages, challenges remain. Low fracture toughness in some composites can still limit their use in long-term applications like permanent fracture fixation devices. Continuous research is crucial to achieve improved biological, tribological, chemical, and mechanical properties that are as identical as possible to original bones or tissues. Future directions include optimizing composite composition and production to improve stress distribution and fiber-matrix bonding for longer-lasting prostheses with fewer issues. Patient-specific solutions and continuous innovation in design and manufacturing (e.g., Functionally Graded Materials where properties vary gradually across the material) are key for the future of biomedical implant materials.

Surface treatment#

Improving biocompatibility, which is defined as a material’s ability to perform with an appropriate host response in a specific application without causing unwanted local or systemic effects, is a critical goal in the design of biomedical implants. The surface of an implant plays a pivotal role in this interaction because it is the first point of contact with the host’s physiological environment, including bone tissue, blood, and proteins. Therefore, various surface treatment methods are employed to enhance the implant’s interaction with biological systems and mitigate potential adverse reactions such as stress shielding, fibrous tissue formation, inflammation, and infection.

Here’s an overview of the types of surface treatments used to improve biocompatibility:

1. Modification of Surface Topography and Porosity Surface topography, including roughness and the presence of porous structures, significantly influences cell adhesion, growth, and bone ingrowth (osseointegration).

  • Porous Structures: The addition of interconnected three-dimensional (3D) pores to an implant’s bulk structure is a widely studied method to enhance biological fixation and early-stage osseointegration. Pores promote cell adhesion, mechanical interlocking between host tissue and the scaffold via bone ingrowth, and facilitate the transport of nutrients and metabolic waste.

    • Pore Characteristics: Pore size, volume fraction porosity, pore-pore interconnectivity, and pore shape strongly influence an implant’s mechanical and biological properties. For instance, a porosity above 60% yields a lower elastic modulus, similar to cancellous bone, which can reduce stress shielding. While smaller pores (e.g., ~100 µm) can be beneficial for chondrocyte attachment, some studies indicate that bone ingrowth in porous metallic structures can be independent of pore size in non-load-bearing conditions. However, optimal pore sizes for effective bone ingrowth and bonding are often cited at a minimum of 200 µm.

    • Materials with Porous Structures:

      • Titanium (Ti) and Ti alloys: Porous Ti structures, including Ti6Al4V, are extensively studied for load-bearing implants and show good osteoblast growth and adhesion. They can be fabricated using additive manufacturing techniques like Electron Beam Melting (EBM) and Selective Laser Melting (SLM) to create tailored porous geometries that match bone elasticity.

      • Tantalum (Ta): Porous tantalum implants, often referred to as “Trabecular Metal,” have demonstrated excellent and rapid bone tissue ingrowth and higher shear strength in vivo, with consistent high ingrowth rates regardless of pore size.

      • Nickel-Titanium (NiTi) (Nitinol): Research on porous NiTi (e.g., pore size 400–900 µm) has shown healthy cell growth within and around the pores.

      • Magnesium (Mg) alloys: Porous magnesium’s in vitro degradation behavior shows that degradation rates can be controlled depending on porosity, with non-toxic degradation products. These materials hold potential for biodegradable implants in bone defects.

  • Mechanical Processing: Techniques like machining, grinding, polishing, and blasting (sandblasting) are used to improve surface topography and roughness, remove contaminants, increase surface area, enhance cell adhesion, and augment mechanical interlocking with implants. Cryogenic machining, for instance, has been shown to improve surface finish and mechanical properties of alloys like Ti-6Al-7Nb and NiTi, leading to better biocompatibility.

2. Electrical Charge Modification Localized charge distribution at the electrostatic surface of a biomaterial can define biological interactions and influence cellular response and bone bonding.

  • Mechanisms: Electrical charge can be stored on implant surfaces through various mechanisms, including the natural DC electrical potential of bone (triggered by mechanical load), external electrothermal polarization (inducing capacitive charge storage), or external electrical triggers applied post-implantation.

  • Benefits: Electrical stimulation has been shown to enhance bone morphogenetic proteins (BMP-2, 4, 5, 6) and increase calcium deposition, leading to faster healing and accelerated osseointegration. Negatively polarized surfaces, such as micro-arc oxidized Ti surfaces or nanotube-modified titanium oxide surfaces, have demonstrated enhanced cellular attachment, growth, and proliferation of osteoblast-like cells, leading to significant increases in newly formed bone area in vivo.

3. Bioactive Coatings Applying coatings to metallic implants is a primary strategy to overcome their inherent bio-inertness, improve tissue integration, and reduce issues like corrosion and ion release.

  • Calcium Phosphates (CaP) and Hydroxyapatite (HAp): As natural bone is composed of about 70% calcium carbonate apatites, CaP-based coatings, especially HAp, are widely used to mimic the inorganic phase of bone, enhancing osteoconductivity, bone bonding, and cellular activity.

    • Types of CaPs: Different classes include Octacalcium Phosphates (OCP), Hydroxyapatite (HAp), α-Tricalcium Phosphates (α-TCP), and β-Tricalcium Phosphates (β-TCP), each with varying Ca/P ratios and resorption rates. HAp, with its slow resorption rate and osteoblast proliferation-inducing properties, is a major component of teeth and bones and a prime coating material.

    • Benefits: CaP coatings promote osseointegration, reduce healing times, and can act as carriers for immobilized drugs or antibacterial agents, preventing secondary infections. Dopants like strontium, silicon, and magnesium can enhance cellular interaction and corrosion resistance.

    • Deposition Techniques: Various methods are used, including:

      • Plasma Spray Deposition: A commercially viable method offering high deposition rates and strong interfacial strength, widely used for HAp coatings.

      • Sol-Gel Method: Involves colloidal suspensions to form thin, often porous, layers at low temperatures, with subsequent annealing to improve bond strength.

      • Electrochemical Deposition: Performed at ambient temperature and pressure, producing uniform coatings with potential for post-deposition calcination to improve bonding.

      • Electrophoretic Deposition (EPD): A flexible and affordable method for depositing uniform films of polymers, ceramics, and composites from colloidal suspensions, offering control over film thickness and deposition rate.

      • Electrospray Deposition (ESD): Sprays precursor solutions onto surfaces to control coating thickness and chemical composition, forming small droplets for fine deposition.

      • Biomimetic Deposition: Involves immersing a metal substrate in a simulated body fluid (SBF) solution to precipitate a bone-like CaP layer.

  • Tantalum (Ta) and Tantalum Oxide Coatings: Ta is known for its remarkable chemical stability, biocompatibility, and corrosion resistance. It forms a protective oxide layer that shields it from corrosive physiological fluids, extending implant life. Ta coatings on Ti implants have shown significantly higher osteoblast response and comparable biocompatibility to HAp-coated Ti.

  • Bioglass and Glass-Ceramics: These bioactive materials form a direct bond with bone, encouraging osteointegration and bone growth. They react with body fluids to form a hydroxycarbonate layer similar to natural bone, facilitating osteoblast attachment and new bone deposition. Glass-ceramics offer improved mechanical strength suitable for load-bearing applications.

  • Polymeric Coatings: Polymers like PEEK, often combined with metals or ceramics, can reduce friction and wear at the implant surface, especially in joint replacements. They can also serve as carriers for drug delivery. For instance, PEEK can be combined with bioglass and silver nanoparticles for antibacterial properties.

  • Hybrid/Composite Coatings: Combining different materials in coatings, such as alumina/zirconia/HAp composites, leverages the benefits of each constituent to achieve high biocompatibility, bioactivity, chemical inertness, and mechanical strength. Calcium phosphate-coated magnesium alloys, for example, have shown enhanced bioactivity.

  • Nano-structured Coatings: The development of nanocoatings, such as nano-structured titanium oxide, HAp, carbon nanotubes, and biopolymer-based nanocoatings, allows for precise functionalization of surfaces to improve biocompatibility, corrosion resistance, and wear resistance.

    • Titania Nanotubes: Rendering Ti implant surfaces with a nanoarchitecture of titania nanotubes enhances bone bonding due to a combined effect of biological fixation into pores and bioactive fixation at the surface.

    • Silver Nanoparticles: Coatings incorporating silver nanoparticles have demonstrated antibacterial properties, reducing the risk of implant-associated infections.

    • Zinc, Niobium, and Tantalum: Incorporating these elements into titanium alloys can suppress inflammation and enhance biocompatibility.

4. Biological Functionalization This involves immobilizing organic molecules native to bone or other bioactive substances on the implant surface to initiate bioactivity and enhance biocompatibility.

  • Molecules Used: This includes collagen or peptide sequences, bone growth factors (e.g., BMP-2, TGF-β), DNA, and bone mineralizing enzymes.

  • Polydopamine Coatings: Inspired by mussels, polydopamine coatings serve as efficient platforms for better cell adhesion and provide osteoinductive and osteoconductive surfaces. They can also act as carrier platforms for other biomolecules like drugs and growth factors.

  • Methods of Immobilization: Biomolecule functionalization is typically achieved via physisorption, physical entrapment (especially on porous surfaces), or covalent bonding.

5. Non-Traditional Subtractive Manufacturing Techniques Beyond conventional mechanical methods, advanced subtractive techniques can also modify surfaces to improve biocompatibility.

  • Electrical Discharge Machining (EDM): EDM processes can create unique surface chemistry (e.g., oxides and carbides), nanoporous structures, and improved topographical features on metallic biomaterials. EDMed surfaces often exhibit enhanced biocompatibility, hardness, and resistance to wear and corrosion. Powder Mixed EDM (PM-EDM) can further enhance surface properties, including biocompatibility, osteoblastic cell activity, and bone-bonding ability, by impregnating fine powder particles like HAp or silver in the dielectric fluid.

  • Waterjet Peening: Abrasiveless waterjet peening can be used to roughen metallic biomaterial surfaces, which can influence cell adhesion and tissue integration.

Future Directions and Challenges Despite significant advancements, challenges persist. Many implants still face issues like stress shielding, corrosion, ion release, and patient-specific immune responses. For instance, nanomaterials, while promising, raise concerns about cytotoxicity, immune activation, and systemic toxicity due to potential detachment and accumulation of nanoparticles in organs. Low fracture toughness can also limit the long-term use of some composites in load-bearing applications.

Future research aims to develop materials that offer a harmonious integration with the human body, combining mechanical excellence with biological compatibility. This includes:

  • Optimizing Composition: Tailoring material composition and processing to improve stress distribution and fiber-matrix bonding for longer-lasting prostheses.

  • Smart Implants: Developing implants embedded with sensors to monitor biomechanical and biochemical changes in real time, offering a new frontier in implant technology.

  • Personalized Solutions: Leveraging additive manufacturing and AI for patient-specific implant designs with tailored porosity and surface modifications, enhancing osseointegration and reducing complications.

  • Addressing Immune Response: Continued exploration of how to mitigate adverse immune responses, especially for new alloys and surface modifications.

The evolution of biomaterials is moving towards “fourth generation” materials, which combine more than two different phases to better control properties like mechanical strength, antibacterial activity, osteoconductivity, degradation rate, and surface characteristics. The ultimate goal is to achieve improved biological, tribological, chemical, and mechanical properties that are as identical as possible to original bones or tissues.
