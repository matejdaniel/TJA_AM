Medical imaging#

What is Medical Imaging?#

Medical imaging is the technique and process of creating visual representations of the interior of a body for clinical analysis and medical intervention, as well as for visualizing the function of some organs or tissues. It encompasses a variety of technologies that generate images by non-invasively “seeing” inside the body. These technologies leverage different physical principles—such as energy absorption, reflection, and emission—to differentiate between various tissue types, from dense bone to soft tissue, providing crucial information for diagnosis, treatment planning, and biomedical research.

The DICOM Standard#

Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine (DICOM) is the universal standard for communicating and managing medical imaging information and related data. It ensures that medical images and their associated information can be created, stored, retrieved, and shared by a wide variety of hardware and software from different manufacturers. A DICOM file consists of two main parts: a header and the image data.

  • DICOM Header: The header contains a standardized set of metadata about the patient and the imaging study. This includes patient information (name, ID, age), study details (modality, date, time, physician), and technical parameters of the image acquisition (e.g., X-ray dose, magnetic field strength, slice thickness). The information is stored as a series of “tags” or key-value pairs, allowing any DICOM-compliant software to correctly interpret the data.

  • DICOM Content: This is the core of the file, containing the raw pixel data that constitutes the image itself. For a 2D image like a chest X-ray, this is a single matrix of pixel values. For 3D modalities like CT or MRI, it’s a series of 2D image slices that can be reconstructed into a 3D volume.

Radiography (X-ray)#

Radiography is an imaging technique using X-rays to view the internal structure of an object. The physical principle is based on the differential attenuation of X-ray photons as they pass through the body. An X-ray source generates a beam of high-energy photons, which is directed at the patient. As the beam travels through different tissues, it is attenuated (absorbed and scattered) to varying degrees. Dense materials like bone absorb many photons and appear white or bright on the resulting image, while less dense materials like soft tissue and air allow more photons to pass through, appearing in shades of gray or black. The transmitted photons are captured by a detector (e.g., film or a digital sensor).

The attenuation is described by the Beer-Lambert law: [I = I_0 e^{-\mu x}] where (I) is the transmitted intensity, (I_0) is the initial intensity, (\mu) is the linear attenuation coefficient of the material, and (x) is the thickness of the material.

Historically, the term skiagraphy (from Greek /skia/, meaning “shadow,” and /graphia/, meaning “writing”) was used for the process of producing a radiographic image—literally “shadow writing.” Today, this term is archaic, and radiography is the standard term.

Fluoroscopy and 3D Motion Analysis#

Fluoroscopy is a type of medical imaging that shows a continuous, real-time X-ray image on a monitor, much like an “X-ray movie.” It’s used to observe moving body structures or to guide interventional procedures like catheter placement. A continuous, low-dose X-ray beam is passed through the body, and the transmitted radiation is captured by an image intensifier or a flat-panel detector, which converts the X-rays into a live video display.

To capture complex 3D motion, two fluoroscopy systems can be run in parallel, a technique often called biplanar videofluoroscopy or Dynamic Stereo X-ray (DSX).

  • Setup: Two X-ray source-and-detector pairs are positioned at a known angle to each other (typically around 90 degrees), both aimed at the subject (e.g., a person walking on a treadmill).

  • Data Acquisition: Both systems simultaneously record fluoroscopic videos of the motion from two different perspectives.

  • 3D Reconstruction: By tracking the 2D position of an anatomical landmark or a surgically implanted marker in both video streams, computer algorithms can calculate its precise 3D coordinates for every frame. This is achieved through triangulation, a method that uses the known geometry of the two imaging systems to find the unique point in 3D space that projects onto the observed 2D locations in each image. Reconstructing the path of multiple points over time yields highly accurate 3D kinematic data about joint motion, which is impossible to obtain from a single 2D projection.

Radiography in Medical Device Design#

Radiography and its dynamic form, fluoroscopy, are fundamental to biomechanics for several reasons:

  • Bone Geometry and Density: It provides clear images of bone structure, allowing for the analysis of skeletal alignment, fracture patterns, and bone density changes.

  • Joint Kinematics: Biplanar fluoroscopy allows researchers to study the dynamic 3D movement and interaction of bones within a joint during activities like walking or jumping.

  • Implant Assessment: It’s used to evaluate the position and integrity of orthopedic implants and to monitor the bone’s response to the implant over time.

Computed Tomography (CT)#

Computed Tomography (CT), sometimes called Computed Axial Tomography (CAT), also uses X-rays but produces cross-sectional images, or “slices,” of the body. Instead of a single, stationary source and detector, a CT scanner features an X-ray source and detector array that rotate around the patient. Hundreds of projections are acquired from different angles as the gantry rotates. This vast amount of data is then processed by a computer to reconstruct a 3D representation of the body.

Data Processing and Back-Projection#

The raw data from a CT scan is a set of attenuation profiles called a sinogram. To convert this sinogram into a meaningful image, a reconstruction algorithm is needed. The most fundamental method is back-projection.

Back-projection is an algorithm that reconstructs a 2D image from a series of 1D projections. Imagine a single projection profile taken through an object. Simple back-projection takes this profile and “smears” it back across the image plane in the direction it was acquired. By doing this for all projections from every angle and summing the results, a rough image of the object emerges.

However, simple back-projection produces heavily blurred images with a characteristic “star” or “spoke” artifact around sharp features. To correct this, a modified technique called Filtered Back-Projection (FBP) is used. Before back-projecting the profiles, a high-pass filter (often a “ramp filter”) is applied to the data in the frequency domain. This filtering step sharpens the edges and removes the blurring, resulting in a clinically useful, high-quality image. FBP is computationally efficient and remains the most common reconstruction method in clinical CT.

CT in Implantable Medical Device Design#

CT is invaluable for designing and evaluating implantable devices, especially in orthopedics.

  • Patient-Specific Design: High-resolution CT scans provide precise 3D data of a patient’s anatomy (e.g., a femur or vertebra). This data can be used in CAD software to design custom-fit implants, improving mechanical stability and patient outcomes.

  • Pre-operative Planning: Surgeons use CT data to simulate surgery, select the optimal size and type of a standard implant, and plan the surgical approach.

  • Post-operative Evaluation: CT scans can verify the exact placement of an implant, check for complications like loosening or breakage, and assess bone growth onto the implant surface (osseointegration).

CT Artefacts#

CT images are susceptible to artefacts that can degrade image quality or mimic pathology. Common artefacts include:

  • Beam Hardening: As the X-ray beam passes through the body, lower-energy photons are preferentially absorbed, “hardening” the beam. This can cause dark streaks or bands between dense objects.

  • Metal Artefacts: The high density of metallic implants causes extreme X-ray attenuation, resulting in severe streaking and shadowing that can obscure surrounding anatomy. Special algorithms (MARS) are used to reduce these effects.

  • Motion Artefacts: Patient movement during the scan causes blurring or ghosting in the reconstructed image.

  • Partial Volume Effect: This occurs when a single voxel contains multiple tissue types (e.g., bone and soft tissue). The voxel is assigned an average attenuation value, blurring the interface between the tissues.

Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) / MRI#

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is a non-invasive imaging technique that does not use ionizing radiation. It is based on the principles of Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR). The physical principle relies on the behavior of atomic nuclei with a non-zero spin, particularly hydrogen protons ((H^+)), which are abundant in the body’s water and fat.

  1. Alignment: The patient is placed in a strong, static magnetic field ((B_0)). This causes the randomly oriented protons to align either parallel or anti-parallel to the field, creating a net longitudinal magnetization ((M_z)).

  2. Precession: The protons also precess (wobble like a spinning top) around the direction of the (B_0) field at a specific frequency, known as the Larmor frequency, given by (\omega_0 = \gamma B_0), where (\gamma) is the gyromagnetic ratio.

  3. Excitation: A radiofrequency (RF) pulse, tuned to the Larmor frequency, is applied. This pulse knocks the net magnetization out of alignment with the (B_0) field and into the transverse plane ((M_{xy})), also forcing the protons to precess in phase.

  4. Signal Detection: When the RF pulse is turned off, the protons begin to relax back to their equilibrium state. As they do, they release the absorbed energy, which induces a detectable signal in a receiver coil. The spatial location of the signal is encoded using magnetic field gradients.

T1 and T2 Relaxation#

The signal contrast in MRI comes from the different relaxation rates of various tissues. Relaxation is characterized by two independent processes: T1 and T2 relaxation.

  • T1 Relaxation (Longitudinal): This is the time it takes for the protons to realign with the main magnetic field ((B_0)). It describes the recovery of longitudinal magnetization ((M_z)). Tissues with short T1 times (e.g., fat) relax quickly and appear bright on T1-weighted images. Tissues with long T1 times (e.g., water/CSF) relax slowly and appear dark.

  • T2 Relaxation (Transverse): This is the time it takes for the precessing protons to dephase, or lose their phase coherence, in the transverse plane. It describes the decay of transverse magnetization ((M_{xy})). Tissues with long T2 times (e.g., water, edema, tumors) dephase slowly and appear bright on T2-weighted images. Tissues with short T2 times (e.g., muscle, cortical bone) dephase quickly and appear dark.

MRI in Medical Device Design#

MRI’s superior soft-tissue contrast makes it essential for designing devices that interact with non-bony tissues.

  • Cardiovascular Devices: MRI is used to visualize blood vessels and heart anatomy to aid in the design of stents, grafts, and artificial heart valves. It can also assess blood flow (MR angiography) and tissue response after implantation.

  • Neurological Devices: For designing devices like deep brain stimulation (DBS) electrodes or spinal cord stimulators, MRI provides detailed maps of the brain and spinal cord, ensuring precise targeting and avoiding critical structures.

  • Material Compatibility: A major consideration is ensuring devices are “MRI-safe” or “MRI-conditional.” Ferromagnetic materials are contraindicated as they can experience dangerous torque and heating. Device designers must use non-ferromagnetic materials like titanium, nitinol, or certain polymers and test them for MRI compatibility.

Ultrasound#

Ultrasound imaging uses high-frequency sound waves (typically 2-18 MHz) and their echoes to create images. A handheld probe called a transducer, which contains piezoelectric crystals, sends short pulses of sound into the body. These sound waves travel through tissues and are reflected back when they hit a boundary between tissues with different acoustic impedances. The transducer detects the returning echoes, and a computer uses the time and intensity of these echoes to create a real-time image. It is widely used in obstetrics, cardiology, and for guiding procedures because it is real-time, portable, inexpensive, and uses no ionizing radiation.

Picture Archiving and Communication System (PACS)#

A Picture Archiving and Communication System (PACS) is a medical imaging technology that provides economical storage and convenient access to images from multiple modalities (e.g., CT, MRI, X-ray).

  • Storage: Medical images, typically in DICOM format, are sent from the imaging modality to a central PACS server. This server uses a large-scale, secure storage system, often involving a combination of fast, short-term storage (like a RAID array) for recent studies and slower, long-term archival storage (like cloud or tape libraries) for older data.

  • Access: Radiologists and clinicians access the images using specialized PACS viewing software on dedicated workstations or through secure web viewers. The system is integrated with the hospital’s information systems, such as the Radiology Information System (RIS) and Hospital Information System (HIS). When a physician needs to view a patient’s images, they query the PACS using patient identifiers. The system retrieves the relevant DICOM files from the archive and sends them to the user’s workstation for display and analysis. This allows for instant, simultaneous access to imaging studies from anywhere in the hospital or even remotely, streamlining the clinical workflow.
